    1. Viktor Jirsa

    Virtual Brain Twins as vehicles to understand pattern formation in the brain

    In the past twenty years, we have made significant progress in creating digital models of an individual’s brain, so called virtual brain twins. By combining brain imaging data with mathematical models, we can predict outcomes more accurately than using each method separately. Our approach has helped us understand normal brain states, their operation and conditions like healthy aging, dementia and schizophrenia. We illustrate the virtual brain workflow along the example of drug resistant epilepsy and demonstrate some basic principles underlying the spatio-temporal pattern formation in brain-like networks.

    2. Sitabhra Sinha

    Seeking Universal Patterns across Connectomes

    Understanding how the brain functions requires identifying general design principles that can provide a conceptual scaffolding for describing the connectome. This talk will describe how (a) by looking across species and (b) looking across many individuals in a species, one can tease out indications of invariant organizational features that shape the dynamics and function of nervous systems. In particular, the talk will focus on the evidence for "modular hierarchy" that involves both modular partitioning and hierarchical layered arrangement of neurons/brain areas across different species, suggesting that nervous systems employ an optimal mix of parallel (modular) and sequential (hierarchic) information processing. It will also briefly touch upon the identification of an universal "backbone network" underlying the human brain obtained by analyzing an ensemble of DTI-based structural connectomes obtained for a wide cross-section of human subjects.

    3. Demian Battaglia

    Why the "D" in "FCD" ? Mechanisms and purpose of dynamic Functional Connectivity

    Functional connectivity describes the coordinated activity of distributed brain regions, typically estimated from correlations in neural time series such as resting-state fMRI. Traditionally, functional networks have been characterised over long time windows or across repeated trials. From a data-driven perspective, however, functional connectivity is inherently dynamic, undergoing continuous and spontaneous reconfiguration—a major source of neural variability, even in the absence of explicit task demands. This ongoing flow of connectivity, and more generally of coordinated brain states, has emerged as a powerful biomarker across a wide range of physiological and pathological conditions, including ageing, sleep deprivation, and neurological disorders. These dynamics reflect a delicate balance between order and disorder, which can be quantified using appropriate markers of network reconfiguration and update dynamics, and are observable across modalities, from invasive recordings in animals and patients to EEG and fMRI in both resting and task conditions. In this talk, drawing on past and ongoing work from our team, I will address the question of why functional connectivity is dynamic. First, in a mechanistic sense: how can flexible yet structured connectivity dynamics emerge from largely fixed large-scale cortical circuits? What insights can be gained from dynamical models that faithfully reproduce subtle but critical features of empirical FCD? This line of inquiry leads us to revisit the prevalent hypothesis that brain networks operate near criticality, and to propose an alternative framework, which we term the critical roaming scenario. I will then turn to a second, more functional interpretation of why: what computational or cognitive advantage might arise from maintaining a continuously evolving connectivity landscape, beyond adaptations explicitly driven by task or context? Using generative models of dynamic functional connectivity with increasing levels of detail, I will explore hypotheses according to which spontaneous reconfiguration enables a compromise between the simultaneous optimisation of integrative and segregative modes of information processing. Overall, our results support the view that dynamic functional connectivity should be regarded not as nuisance variability to be averaged out, but as a structured computational resource actively exploited by the brain.

    4. James Roberts

    Modelling brain dynamics on long time scales

    Brain activity unfolds on an extremely wide range of time scales, though most effort to date has focused on studying relatively short time scales of milliseconds to minutes. In models, different brain states often map to different sets of parameter values, but then questions remain on how models parsimoniously explain transitions between different states, and how brain activity matures and ages. This talk will present work on modelling “slow” variables beyond the traditional neuronal ones. For example there are slow changes on the order of hours in metabolic and ionic availability, and circadian and homeostatic processes, and on longer time scales still these dynamics evolve across the lifespan. Applications include recovery from hypoxic injury and the maturation of sleep patterns. Many possibilities remain to be pursued, discussion of which will hopefully inspire new models explaining dynamics on time scales of hours, days, months, and years.

    5. Satu Palva

    Critical brain dynamics of network oscillations

    Network-oscillations are fundamental for human cognition. Especially, phase synchronization of neuronal oscillations is considered as a mechanism for routing information processing across neuronal circuits. Yet, there is wide variability of both individual coupling levels and their spatio-temporal patterns. Using computational modelling and experimental MEG data, we have shown that the variability can be explained in the framework of brain criticality by individual operating points along an extended critical-like dynamics – the Griffiths Phase. I will discuss these results and propose that the extended critical regime yields functional advantages of criticality over a wider range of operating point values. This could lead to individual differences in neural circuit level and behavioral and cognitive performance.

    6. Parul Verma

    Time-varying spectral graph model describes the dynamics of abnormal neural oscillations in Alzheimer's disease

    Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is associated with marked changes in brain rhythms, often reflected as shifts in spectral content and altered excitatory-inhibitory balance of neural activity. However, how such oscillatory changes relate to biophysical processes at both local (neural population level) and global (network level) scales of brain organization remains poorly understood. In this work, we examine a time-varying modeling framework that links brain oscillatory power to underlying network structure, using noninvasive neural recordings. This approach enables characterization of dynamic interactions between large-scale network properties and local neural population dynamics. Our results indicate that AD brains tend to occupy distinct dynamical regimes characterized by altered network coordination and regional activity patterns. Furthermore, we show that network dynamics not only reflect important features that distinguish AD from controls but also relate to cognitive decline in AD patients. Together, these findings provide a unifying biophysical perspective on oscillatory dysfunction in AD and offer a general framework for studying multiscale brain dynamics and their relevance to clinical outcomes.

    ## Posters

    The event will be held at the newly constructed Sinclairs Palace Retreat in the city of Udaipur. The city of Udaipur was founded by Maharana Udai Singh II in 1559, and is often called the ''City of Lakes'' for its picturesque water bodies like Lake Pichola and Fatehsagar. Known for its grand palaces, forts, and vibrant culture, Udaipur served as the capital of the Mewar kingdom and remains a major cultural and tourist center in Rajasthan.

    There are several tourist attractions nearby. This includes Haldighati, a narrow mountain pass, which is known for the famous Battle of Haldighati fought in 1576 between Maharana Pratap of Mewar and the Mughal army led by Man Singh. The pass, named after its turmeric-colored soil, stands as a symbol of Rajput valor and resistance.
